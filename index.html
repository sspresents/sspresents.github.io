About Screen Sustain

Screen Sustain is an English 1102 course with the goal of fostering environmental discussions on the Georgia Tech campus. As the name suggests, we intend to initiate conversation by screening a film because films contain complex environments, both in physical settings and characters’ attitudes. Character traits and attitudes such as wastefulness and indifference towards the environment matter just as much as the setting, postapocalyptic or not, itself. Screen Sustain has particular interest in sustainability. Check out our class blog for posts about the topic throughout the course here.
To this end, we have viewed and discussed several possible films, before finally selecting The Lorax (2012).

What is Ecocinema?

Defined broadly, ecocinema is any film that addresses the environment—either cultural or physical. By this definition, just about any film can be considered ecocinema. The Lorax, while certainly not conventional ecocinema, surpasses the previous, basic definition through its theme that industrialism harms the environment.

Through this theme, The Lorax directly addresses sustainability. An entrepreneur, the Once-ler, sells “thneeds,” a product made from the Truffala Tree. As his business grows, the Once-ler must cut down progressively larger amounts of trees—which he does despite the Lorax’s protestations and the damage to the ecosystem—until there are no more trees.

This newer adaptation adds more to this Dr. Seuss classic, such as a bustling, walled-in city and a second, less repentant entrepreneur. These additions especially are intended to represent modern society, albeit in a rather hyperbolized manner. Yet, in the opening sequence, the Lorax claims the events of the film actually happened, and this too is true. Industries require wood, leading to deforestation; artificial plants replace less convenient living ones; people often act indifferently to issues not in their backyard. The ultimatum “unless [someone like you cares a whole awful lot, nothing is going to get better]” applies just as much to the world of the Lorax as it does to us.
